1.Introduction

Not only is there no consensus on what interaction means, or it’s role of visualization, the notion of interaction has only recently been outlined for Human Computer Interaction (HCI) and visualization researchers do not necessarily know how to apply the concepts of interaction in their own data- oriented practices. While interaction occurs at every stage of the visualization process, the community has paid less attention to the interaction aspect of the pipeline. This paper proposes crafting a definition for interaction for visualization, by combining researcher’s input and the view of interaction in the HCI community.

2.Research Metholodologies

The paper lists it’s research methodologies. Which include restrictions, expert papers, and stats.

3.The Visualization View of Interation.

This section of the paper defines the view of interaction in visualization based on 59 papers reviewed.

3.1. Definitions of Interaction for Visualization

The researchers considered the 32 papers with definitions, sorting them by whether they define interaction implicitly or explicitly.

3.1.1 What Interaction for Visualization must Involve

The definition papers imply the need for one entity (external), and two actions: external and internal.

External Entities: User and data are the two most cited entities.

External Actions: Interaction is described as “a dialogue between the user and the visualization system made of action-reaction pairs.” The user provides input and the system returns an output that is perceived by the user.

Internal Actions: Interaction requires the cognitive act of the user processing the data for themselves.

3.1.2 What Interaction for Visualization Can Involve

Interaction for visualization can involve additional external and internal entities, and additional external and internal actions.

External Entities: Can involve physical objects such as mouse, pen, and a variety of body movements. Modalities such as gaze and body movements tend to be “overlooked”. Interaction involves the environment it takes place.

Internal Entities: User’s prior knowledge and skills are involved when interacting with a system. Interactions might occur without intent.

External Actions: User actions can change data and the presentation of information. A user can also perform actions that do not affect the data visualization.

Internal Actions: Users can interact with data in their minds, including processing and reasoning.

3.1.3 Properties of Interaction

Interaction for visualization is goal-orientated, contains semantics and preserves the following:

Granularity: Interaction contains multiple levels of granularity.

Continuous vs. Discrete: Interactions can be continuous (happen over a span of time) and discrete (in which action and reaction occur in a distinct manner), interactions can have different continuity – a continuous action can have a discrete reaction.

Direct vs Indirect: Rather than think of interactions as direct or indirect, we should consider interactions to have varying degrees of direct or indirectness.

3.1.4. Terms Related to Interaction

Interaction, interaction techniques, interactivity, and science of interaction are distinct from each other.

nteraction technique refers to the wats users can interact – in terms of hardware and software – it also does not imply intent. Interaction refers to the action-reaction between user and system, while Interactivity refers to the feel, properties, and quality of interaction. Science of Interaction is the study of knowledge-creating through interaction.

3.2. Reported Benefits and Critiques on Interaction

Interaction is seen as a way to allow users to be in control of information spaces, using interaction to learn, test and share information. Interaction helps make visualizations more accessible.

Frustrations around interaction:
- interaction is not often the focus of visualization research.
- interaction research focuses on implementation, not designing
- there is limited consideration for different forms of interaction compared to “classic desktop-mouse- keyboard setups”
- visualization systems are not flexible enough for users to do a considerable number of things surrounding the data – including organizing and annotating data.
- lack of flexibility is caused by technical challenges, costs, and lack of theoretical foundations.
